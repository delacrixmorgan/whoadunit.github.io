The core distinction

MP vs ADUN

Malaysia has two tiers of elected representatives. You have both. Each does different things. Here's the simple version.

Federal · National
Member of Parliament (MP)

Sits in Dewan Rakyat in Kuala Lumpur. Votes on national laws, budgets, and federal policies that apply to all 32 million Malaysians.

222
Federal constituencies
State · Local
State Assemblymember (ADUN)

Sits in the State Assembly in your state capital. Handles local governance — roads, drains, land, councils — the everyday stuff in your neighbourhood.

which one

Contact your ADUN
The drain outside your house has been blocked for weeks
Local infrastructure is managed by state councils, which the ADUN liaises with.

Contact your MP
Fuel prices rose after the latest budget announcement
Subsidies and fuel pricing are federal policies. Your MP voted on the budget.

The structure

How Malaysia's
parliament works

Malaysia is a constitutional monarchy with a bicameral parliament. Here's the stack, from top to bottom.

1
Yang di-Pertuan Agong (King)
Constitutional monarch, elected by the Conference of Rulers on a 5-year rotation from the 9 royal state rulers.
70
Dewan Negara (Senate)
Upper house. 44 appointed by the King, 26 elected by state assemblies. Can delay but not block bills.
222
Dewan Rakyat (House of Representatives)
Lower house. 222 elected MPs. The dominant chamber — forms the government and passes laws. Your MP sits here.
576
Dewan Undangan Negeri (State Assemblies)
11 state assemblies across Peninsular Malaysia, Sabah and Sarawak. 576 ADUNs represent constituencies within each state.

Automatic voter registration
Malaysia introduced automatic voter registration in 2021

All citizens aged 18 and above are automatically registered. No action needed — just check your registration status at the SPR website before the next election.

Common questions

Frequently
asked

Can I contact my MP even if I didn't vote for them?
Yes — absolutely. Your MP represents every constituent in the federal seat, regardless of how they voted. They are paid by public funds and accountable to all voters in their area. Don't let political affiliation stop you from reaching out.
What's the difference between a service centre and Parliament?
Service centres (pusat khidmat) are local offices your MP or ADUN sets up in the constituency for residents to visit. Parliament and the State Assembly are where they vote on laws. For local problems, go to the service centre. For national policy, write to Parliament.
My area has 3 ADUNs but only 1 MP. Why?
Federal constituencies are larger than state constituencies. One federal seat (P001, P002…) covers multiple state seats (N01, N02, N03…). So you'll always have exactly one MP, but you may have 2–4 ADUNs depending on how many state seats fall inside your federal seat.
What if my MP or ADUN never responds?
Try multiple channels: email, phone, social media, and in person at the service centre. If you still get no response, document it — this is valuable information for elections. You can also raise it publicly via local community groups or journalists.
Can Federal Territory residents vote in state elections?
No. Kuala Lumpur, Putrajaya and Labuan are Federal Territories — they are administered by the federal government, not a state government. Residents do not have ADUNs or a State Assembly. They vote only in the General Election for their MP.
What is a by-election?
A by-election is called when a seat becomes vacant mid-term — due to the representative's death, resignation, or disqualification. Only the voters in that specific constituency vote. By-elections are sometimes used as political barometers between general elections.
